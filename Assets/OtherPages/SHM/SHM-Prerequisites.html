A quick revision of the building blocks of geometry


Of course we are taking the Euclidean view and not the modern set theoretic view
Point: A point is that which has position but no magnitude.

Basically what it means is that the only property that a point has is its position. It has no property called length or area or volume â€“ it only has position (similar to the case that a 'colour' has no taste, it has only the property of 'colour' like green, red etc.;
or like the case that a 'sunset' has no mass, it can have time or brightness values but it is meaningless to ask for the mass of a sunset).
If you find the above difficult, think of a point as a very very small circle (which becomes samller when you try to zoom in on it)

Try to zoom in on the point shown in the next slide as a blue circle or dot. You will find that although you are zooming in, the size of the dot does not change because it is a point and it is not supposed to have a 'size'. the dot is used for our convenience to locate the point, because an actual theoretical point will not be visible to us.
You will also notice numbers associated with the point given in brackets. The position of a single entity in an infinite universe is again meaningless (try to imagine a point in empty space, there is no meaning in saying that it is here or it is there as we cannot say anything about its position because we have no landmark/marker/reference to compare it with). Thus, if we need to specify position of a point, we have to specify it with respect to a reference i.e. another point which we take to be fixed for all time unlike our original point which can move. This fixed point or landmark is the origin (the point 0 in the graph).

Line Segment: A straight line segment is a collection of points (called locus) along the straight path between two points. It has only the property of length, it does not have any width or thickness. Later we will see that a few other properties can be assigned to it, depending on context.

To go from the concept of a point to a line, think of a line as a large collection of points next to each other. The idea of length cannot be simplied further and it is assumed that everyone agrees what it means (we assume that either the concept of distance is understood or the concept of straightness in defining a line segment).
A line is enclosed by two points at its ends.

Line: A line is a an extended line segment and extends to infinity on both sides of the associated line segment.

It has infinite length, what I mean to say here is that a line also has the property of length but its value is infinite.
Note we can say that both its ends are at infinity with respect to any other point that we can see.

Ray: A ray is also an extended line segment and extends to infinity but only on one side of the associated line segment.

It also has infinite length but one of its ends is at a finite distance from some other point that we can see.

Dimension of geometrical objects

We can associate the concept of dimension to geometric objects (similar to the idea that we can associate brightness or darkness to colour values. Example: yellow can be considered as bright whereas brown can be considered as a dark colour).

You will be surpised to know that there are many ways of defining dimension, but that is not relevant for now. We will be non-committal at this point and be guided by intuition.

A single point is zero dimensional i.e. the smallest type of space required to contain a single point is called zero dimensional.
We say that a point is a geometric object having zero dimension.

Since a single point does not have any size, no space is required to completely contain it and hence it is zero dimensional. smallest type of space Please understand I am over-simplifying.

The smallest type of space required to contain two points (not on top of each other) is called one dimensional space.

Any number of points (more than two) arranged in particular way can also be contained in one dimension (but not all possible arrangment of many points)


A line is one dimensional i.e. the kind of space required to completely contain a single line, line segment or ray is called one-dimensional.
We say that a line is a geometric object having one dimension.

A line, line segment or ray is that particular arrangment of points that can be contained in one dimension.

Three points that do not lie on a single line cannot be contained in one dimensional space, hence, we invent a new type of space called two dimensional space, which can contain two lines.
Similarly, two lines which are not on top of each other cannot be contained in one dimensional space and require two dimensional space to be completely contained.

It turns out that any number of lines can be accomodated in two dimensional space and therefore any geometrical shape that is made of lines can be accomodated in two dimensions.

If any number of lines and points can be accomodated in two dimensional space, then how do we get to three dimensions?
The answer lies in angles.

Hint: If we impose the condition of getting three lines such that all three lines have the same angle with respect to each other, then such an arrangment is not possible in two dimensional space and we need to go to three dimensional space to make such an arrangment possible. (or we could have taken four points and asked to make the distance between them the same; such an arrangment will also require three dimensions)

Angle: A plane angle is the inclination of two lines to one another in a plane.

Note that parallel lines are taken to have zero angle and non-parallel lines will always meet a some point.
Inclination between two lines can be understood as a measure of how close together the two lines are; because a line is composed of many points, there is directly obvious unique answer.

Two lines inclined in such a way that it has the most symmetry (without being parallel) is taken to have an angle of 90 degrees. Defining 1 degree also requires the help of symmetry.

Two dimension also allows us to define a new type of thing called area. Physically you can think of it as the region inside a closed geometrical object.
The idea of region is expected to be understood and so also closed.
We cannot have a closed figure with only two line segments, we need at least three line segments to form a closed figure and this is known as a triangle (because this figure has three unique angles).

A triangle is made of three line segments, has three angles and has three points (called vertices; singular of which is vertex) where pairs of the lines meet.

In your interactive exploration of a triangle in the previous slide, you must have noticed that when you move one of the points or vertices of a triangle, then the value of all angles (or at least two angles) change; this shows that the value of the angles of a triangle are connected.
This connection is sometimes expressed as the fact that the sum of the three angles of a triangle is $ 180^\circ$.
If the angles are connected, then it is reasonable to ask if the length of the line segments (called sides) of a triangle connected?

Are the length of the sides of a triangle connected?

Indeed they are. This is slightly more complicated and expressed as an inequality:
"the sum of the lengths of any 2 sides of a triangle is greater than the third side"

In your interactive exploration of a triangle in one of the previous slides, you must have noticed that when you move one of the points or vertices of a triangle, then the value of the length of the two sides touching the point changes.

It is found that the complicated relationship among the sides of a triangle becomes simple for one particular type of triangle: the so-called right triangle.

This simpler relationship is the famous Pythagorean theorem (also called Pythagoras' theorem)

A right triangle is a triangle in which one angle is $ 90^\circ$.
Since, the total is to be $180^\circ$, then obviously the other two are less than $90^\circ$.
The longest side of a right triangle is called the hypotenuse; the side opposite to $90^\circ$ angle is called the opposite side and the remaining side is called the adjacent side.

Pythagoras' theorem

"the square of the longest side of a triangle is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two smaller sides"

If you played nicely with the right triangle in the previous slide, then you should have observed:

  • That, since we have forced one of the angles to be $90^\circ$, therefore now changing any point/vertex only changes the value of two angles.
  • There is a pattern in how the sides of the right triangle change (obviously we had the Pythagoras' theorem, but still).
  • If the sides of a right-triangle are changed while keeping the angles same, then the ratio of the different sides remains same (you will need you use a calculator/do calculation to check).
There appears to be a curious relationship between the ratio of the sides and the angles of a right-triangle.
This relationship cannot be expressed by any form of finite polynomial function (or any primitive/simple function) and therefore we invent a new functions.

Since there are three sides let us represent them by $h, p$ and $b$.
$h$-for hypotenuse;
$p$ for opposite side (it is weird to use symbol $o$ and $p$ is sometimes also called perpendicular);
and, finally $b$ for the adjacent side (also called the known as the base hence $b$).
In how many ways can three things ($h, p$ and $b$) be arranged taking two at a time (order does not matter)?

Let us find out
  1. $\quad p$ and $h$
  2. $\quad b$ and $h$
  3. $\quad p$ and $b$

Thus, there are three ways.
Of course we could have use the mathematical method of combination and permutations but why bother for such a simple problem that can be solved by hand.
Each of the three ratios that we got previously is given a name and is considered a function (just like $f(x)=x^2$ is a function)

  1. the ratio $\frac{p}{h}$ is called sine abbreviated as sin>.
  2. the ratio $\frac{b}{h}$ is called cosine abbreviated as cos.
  3. the ratio $\frac{p}{b}$ is called tangent abbreviated as tan.

Remember that the ratio of the sides of a right-triangle depends only on the angle formed by the two sides, hence, sin, cos and tan are functions of angle.
Just like an unknown number is usually denoted by $x$, an unknown angle is usually denote by the Greek letter $\theta$.
The three ratios sin, cos and tan are called trigonometric ratios (and when used with an unknown/variable angle $\theta$ are called trigonometric functions).
Thus,
$sin(\theta) = \frac{p}{h} \quad \quad$ $cos(\theta) = \frac{b}{h} \quad \quad$ $tan(\theta) = \frac{p}{b}$

Note that if we forget about the sides for some time, then we have just got 3 brand new functions.
Example:let $f(\theta) = sin(\theta)$.
Here, $f(\theta)$ is a function of $\theta$ just like $f(x) = x^2$ is a function of $x$.
Now I come to the end of this section and reveal a well-known secret!
The two functions - sine and consine that we just defined are the only simple functions that are repetitive in nature
that is, on slowly changing the value of $\theta$, the value of $sin(\theta)$ as well as $ cos(\theta)$ repeats after a certain interval.
It turns out that any other (no matter how complicated) repetitive function can be always written as a sum of sine and consine functions: this is the basis for Fourier series/analysis and we will not go into that for the time being.
